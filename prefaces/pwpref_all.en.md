## Page 02 — Foreword, 1 (vol. 1)

<sub>Source (scan): [pw1-000-2.png](https://sanskrit-lexicon.uni-koeln.de/scans/csldev/csldoc/build/dictionaries/prefaces/pwpref/pwpref02.html)</sub>

FOREWORD.

Alongside the so-called Petersburg Dictionary in seven volumes, completed a few years ago, it seemed fitting to produce a shorter treatment, one that would meet the needs of beginners and of those users for whom the apparatus given there is too rich. This was at the same time an opportunity to provide for the dictionary itself the additions and improvements possible at the present moment. When the undersigned made this intention known to his friends Roth, Kern, Stenzler and Weber, he was not only encouraged by them to do so, but also received the assurance that they would gladly further the new undertaking in every way. They have fulfilled the promise they gave most splendidly, improving more and adding more new material than I could and might have expected. None of them failed to contribute improvements, but of new material Roth and Kern gave the most. Other scholars too hastened to deliver their larger or smaller contributions. With gratitude I recall Messrs. C. Cappeller, B. Delbrück, R. Garbe, K. Geldner, J. Jolly, A. Leskien, J. Muir, R. Pischel, A. Schiefner, Leopold Schröder and W. O. E. Windisch; but the greatest thanks are always due to the four friends named first.

The new dictionary was thus not meant to supplant the older one, but to appear in a more or less dependent relationship to it. It was to improve where something was to be improved, and to add where something was lacking, while, by the omission of all the citations and passages given there, constantly reminding the reader that the larger dictionary must remain the principal source. Everyone will soon become aware for himself whether he can be content with the shorter work, or whether he must also reach for the more detailed one; but whoever possesses the latter will not wish to do without the former, because, as has already been remarked above, it corrects and supplements, because it brings together what is scattered in various places, and finally because it will be much more convenient in use.

Accented are only those words which occur in accented texts. Where the accent of a word appearing in an accented text could not be determined because it stands here only in the vocative, then the book in which the word first appears has always been named. A word, a meaning, a construction or a gender hitherto adduced only by grammarians or lexicographers has been marked with \*. But when the grammarian or lexicographer does not simply hand down, or perhaps present to us words or whole sentences invented merely ad hoc by himself, but employs a word as an independent author, then such a word has been regarded as one attested in the literature. The Bhaṭṭikâvja, cited here and there, has, as a mere exemplification of Pâṇini's grammar, no claim to the name of an independent literary work. A citation adduced earlier will as a rule be found repeated only when it has been placed under another meaning of the word or has been corrected. Here and there a citation is only apparently new, insofar as merely a better edition or a more accessible book has been substituted; as a rule, however, this happens only when distrust gave occasion for a renewed examination of a passage. That checking has often been carried out, one will soon be able to convince oneself; but that not all passages have been looked at once more, I hardly need to mention. With books that have complete indices, the numbers have not been added without need, especially in the later sheets. The missing attestations for newly admitted compounds will be found in the larger work either under the first or under the second word. Words in which व and ब, or श, ष and स interchange, are listed only in the older or better attested spelling.
